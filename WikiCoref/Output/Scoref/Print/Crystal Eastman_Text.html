(4) Her parents , Samuel Elijah Eastman and Annis Bertha Ford , were both Congregational Church clergy , and together served as pastors at the church of Thomas K. Beecher near Elmira , New York .

(8) She was the sister of the socialist activist Max Eastman , with whom she was quite close throughout her life .

(10) The group , including Ida Rauh , Inez Milholland , Floyd Dell , and Doris Stevens , also spent summers and weekends in Croton on the Hudson .

(12) Receiving her law degree from New York University Law School , she graduated second in the class of 1907 .

(14) Her report , Work Accidents and the Law -LRB- 1910 -RRB- , became a classic and resulted in the first workers ' compensation law , which she drafted while serving on a New York state commission .

(16) She was at one time called the `` most dangerous woman in America , '' due to her free-love idealism and outspoken nature .
(17) During a brief marriage to Wallace J. Benedict which ended in divorce , Eastman lived in Milwaukee and managed the unsuccessful 1912 Wisconsin suffrage battle .
(18) When she returned east in 1913 , she joined Alice Paul , Lucy Burns , and others in founding the militant Congressional Union , which became the National Woman 's Party .
(19) After the passage of the 19th Amendment gave women the vote in 1920 , Eastman and three others wrote the Equal Rights Amendment , first introduced in 1923 .
(20) One of the few socialists to endorse the ERA , she warned that protective legislation for women would mean only discrimination against women .
(21) Eastman claimed that one could assess the importance of the ERA by the intensity of the opposition to it , but she felt that it was still a struggle worth fighting .
(22) During World War I , Eastman was one of the founders of the Woman 's Peace Party , soon joined by Jane Addams , Lillian D. Wald , and others .
(23) She served as president of the New York branch .
(24) Renamed the Women 's International League for Peace and Freedom in 1921 , it remains the oldest extant women 's peace organization .
(25) Eastman also became executive director of the American Union Against Militarism , which lobbied against America 's entrance into the European war and more successfully against war with Mexico in 1916 , sought to remove profiteering from arms manufacturing , and campaigned against conscription and imperial adventures .
(26) When the United States entered World War I , Eastman organized with Roger Baldwin and Norman Thomas the National Civil Liberties Bureau to protect conscientious objectors , or in her words : `` To maintain something over here that will be worth coming back to when the weary war is over '' .
(27) The NCLB grew into the American Civil Liberties Union , with Baldwin at the head and Eastman functioning as attorney-in-charge .
(28) Eastman is credited as a founding member of the ACLU , but her role as founder of the NCLB may have been largely ignored by posterity due to her personal differences with Baldwin .
(29) In 1916 Eastman married the British editor and antiwar activist Walter Fuller .
(30) They had two children , Jeffrey and Annis .
(31) They worked together until the end of the war , when he returned to England to find work .
(32) In 1917 , Eastman co-founded a radical journal of politics , art , and literature , The Liberator , with her brother Max .

(34) After the war , Eastman organized the First Feminist Congress in 1919 .
(35) She commuted between London to be with her husband , and New York , where she was blacklisted and thus rendered unemployable during the Red Scare of 1919-1920 .
(36) During the 1920s her only paid work was as a columnist for feminist journals , notably Equal Rights and Time and Tide .
(37) Eastman claimed that `` life was a big battle for the complete feminist , '' but she was convinced that the complete feminist would someday achieve total victory .
